Tool shed

Published on Wed 30 January 2013 under

"Use the right tool for the job" - a worn but still useful adage. Applying it in the context of software development is not trivial, however:

  • the number of tools is huge
  • they are difficult to discover
  • they are difficult to compare
  • they are difficult to match to a problem/need

Some niches have a dominant tool (JUnit) or several dominant tools with significant philosophical differences (Ant vs. Maven vs. Gradle). These offer strong communities and comprehensive documentation. On the other hand, they can suffer from featuritis and backward compatibility issues. They can be overwhelming, complex and needlessly heavyweight for many tasks.

Then there are the trendy, "hot" tools, the Next Big Things. They carry a risk of ending up as yet-another-whatever, one needs to compensate for the hype when examining and evaluating them. Their main selling point tends to be simplicity which tends to diminish once real-life deployments reveal all the corner cases.

Some niches have no canonical choice, quick tryouts may be the best approach there.

What's needed is an effective way to choose tools based on the problem to be solved. What's also needed is a time budget for learning and experimenting with the tools (even those one has been using for some time). Cheat sheets are a good thing, so is memory training.
