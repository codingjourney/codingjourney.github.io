In Defence of Breaking Things

Published on Thu 21 March 2013 under

The ossification of programming languages is a serious problem with real-world consequences. The need for backward-compatibility results in feature bloat. Different communities deal with it in different ways. Most adapt (deprecation warnings, coding standards). Three languages stand out:

  • Scala: "if it's broke, do fix it". Users must be ready to refactor due to language improvement, they get better code in return.
  • C: "we do not change". Limited, well-defined scope. Self-selecting community of skeptical curmudgeons. No trust in easy solutions to hard problems.
  • Python: "you can bring a horse to water but you can't make him drink". Careful migration to a big-bang incompatible release. Still not done.

These approaches provide lessons:

  • Scala: There are better ways of doing things. There are people willing to adapt to a changing language. Hope this holds up as the body of code grows.
  • C: The closer one is to the metal, the less evolution one needs (yeah but what about multi-core?). Stability breeds mature codebases and transferable skills. Simplicity keeps code honest.
  • Python: evolving a language with lots of legacy code is slow. Supporting the legacy codebase is The Right Thing but creates a schism.

The core question is what to do when a tool serves well but reaches its limits. Sometimes you just need to switch tools (ZeroMQ, Couchbase). Too often, people think brand-new tools are needed (NIH syndrome, creative impulse). Learning the wheel is more boring than re-inventing it. Ada is an extreme case of un-evolution: valuable, stable, unused (presumably painful to work with).
